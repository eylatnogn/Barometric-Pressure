A Complete Beginner's Guide

How to read the app and what every number means - written for someone who has never opened it before.

In one sentence

PressureSense watches the barometric (air) pressure where you are and helps you see how it lines up with how you feel - so dizziness, vertigo, and migraine days become a little more predictable.

Please read this first

This app is a personal wellness tracker, not a medical device. It does not diagnose anything or replace your doctor. Use it to spot your own patterns and to share an export with a clinician - not to make medical decisions on its own.

Why air pressure?

The air around us has weight, and that weight - barometric pressure - constantly rises and falls as weather systems move through. Many people with vestibular disorders, vertigo, Meniere's, or migraine find that their symptoms flare when the pressure changes, especially when it drops quickly before a storm.

You can't feel the pressure directly, but your inner ear and sinuses can react to it. PressureSense turns that invisible change into a number and a picture you can actually watch - and warns you when a big swing is on the way, so you can rest or take medication ahead of it.

The single most important idea

It is usually not the pressure being high or low that bothers people - it's how fast it changes. A steady day, high or low, is usually calm. A rapid drop is the classic trigger. That's why this app shows you the change, not just the number.

Getting started (first 30 seconds)

  • 1. Set your location. The first time you open the app, tap "Use my current location" (easiest) or type a city name and pick it from the list. The weather is fetched for that spot.
  • 2. Choose your units (optional). You can switch pressure between hPa and inHg, and temperature between C and F. Pick whatever looks familiar - it changes nothing except the labels.
  • 3. That's it. The Now screen fills in. Come back anytime; it remembers your location and your symptom log on your device.

Reading the "Now" screen

This is where most of the numbers live. Here's each one, top to bottom.

The big number - current pressure

The large figure is the air pressure right now, in your chosen unit.

  • hPa (hectopascals) is the worldwide standard. ~1013 hPa is the global average at sea level. Roughly: above 1023 = high/settled, around 1013 = average, below 1000 = low/unsettled.
  • inHg (inches of mercury) is common in the US. ~29.92 inHg is the average. Higher is calmer, lower is stormier.

The trend pill - which way it's heading

Just under the big number is a colored pill with an arrow. It compares now to 6 hours ago:

RisingPressure climbing. Weather often improving. Rapid rises can still be a trigger for some.
SteadyLittle change - usually the calmest, most comfortable window.
FallingPressure dropping. The most common symptom trigger - worth paying attention to.

The pill also shows the exact change, e.g. "Falling - -3.4 hPa / 6h".

Change - 3 hr / 6 hr / 24 hr

These three boxes are the heart of the app. Each shows how much the pressure has moved over that time window. A red, negative number means it fell; an amber, positive number means it rose.

Rule of thumb

A change of about 5-6 hPa or more over a few hours is the kind of swing that sensitive people often notice. Small wobbles of a point or two are normal and usually nothing.

The alert banner (top of the screen)

A colored bar gives a plain-language heads-up about the next 12 hours:

Green / calmPressure looks stable. A good window if you're sensitive to change.
Amber / cautionA notable rise is forecast. Rapid changes can trigger symptoms - take it easy.
Red / heads-upA notable drop is forecast. Consider preparing ahead - rest, hydration, or medication as your clinician advises.

Temperature, Humidity, Conditions

Supporting weather context: the current temperature, the relative humidity as a percentage, and a short description of the sky (Clear, Overcast, Light rain, and so on). These don't drive the alerts - they just help you picture the day.

The square pressure graph

The chart shows the last 24 hours of pressure as a line. A dotted vertical line marks now. A line sloping downhill toward "now" means falling pressure; uphill means rising. The numbers up the left side are pressure values; the labels along the bottom are times.

The "Forecast" tab

Same square graph, but looking 48 hours ahead. Below it is a list that breaks the time into roughly 6-hour blocks. Each row tells you the trend and the size of the change:

Steady - calm windowLittle movement expected. The easy stretches to plan demanding activities.
Gentle rise / fallA modest change is coming. Usually mild, but worth noticing if you're sensitive.
Notable drop / Sharp riseA bigger swing. The number on the right (e.g. -4.2) is how many units it moves in that block.

Use this to plan ahead: if a notable drop is coming tomorrow afternoon, you can take it easy beforehand instead of being caught off guard.

The "Log" tab

This is how the app learns your patterns. Whenever you feel something (or feel fine), make a quick entry:

  • Severity slider (0-10): 0 is "none," 10 is "severe." Just estimate - consistency matters more than precision.
  • Symptom chips: tap any that apply (Dizziness, Vertigo, Headache, Nausea, Ear pressure, and so on). Tap again to unselect.
  • Notes: optional free text - "woke up dizzy," "took medication," anything useful to future-you.
  • Save entry: the app automatically attaches the current pressure, so you never have to write it down.

Tip

Log the good days too, not just the bad ones. The app finds patterns by comparing your better and worse days - it needs both to learn anything.

Export: the "Export" button saves your whole log as a CSV spreadsheet - handy to bring to a doctor's appointment. Your entries live only on your own device; nothing is uploaded anywhere.

The "Trends" tab

This is the payoff. The square chart draws the recent pressure line, and overlays your logged entries as colored dots:

  • Each dot sits at the time you logged it. Bigger, redder dots = higher severity; small green dots = mild.
  • Over a few weeks you may *see* the dots cluster on the downhill slopes - that's your personal pattern emerging.

Below the chart, a short written summary appears once you have a handful of entries, for example: "On your worse days, pressure averaged 8 hPa lower than on your good days." It also notes your most-logged symptom. This is a description of your data, not a diagnosis.

Getting the most out of it

  • Check it in the morning and glance at the alert banner to see what kind of day to expect.
  • Log little and often. A 10-second entry whenever you feel a change builds the picture fastest.
  • Give it a few weeks. One or two entries can't show a pattern; a month of them often can.
  • Add it to your home screen. In your phone browser, choose "Add to Home Screen" and it opens like a normal app, full-screen, even offline.
  • Bring the export to appointments. A clinician can do more with a month of logged data than with "I think the weather affects me."
